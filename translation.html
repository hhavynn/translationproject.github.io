Jiangzhou-deputy-chief-green-shirt-wet. 		At Xunyang River, I bade farewell to the guests,
Maple leaves rustle in the autumn evening.
The host and guests embark the boat,
with no music, toasting and drinking.
Drunk with nothing but misery we part,
with the moonlight pervading the river.
Suddenly we hear the sound of Pipa,
Host and guest, enthralled by the player.
Following the sound we wanted to find the source,
the music stops, with hesitation to speak.
Approaching with our boat we yearned to meet,
with wine and turned-on lights, restarting the feast.
With invites after invites she finally came out.
The Pipa still half covering her face,
Turning axles, plucking strings, producing few sounds,
With no melodies but feelings we can trace.
Vibrating strings seem to hide her melancholic thoughts
As if mourning for her unfinished ambitions.
She bowed her head, casually plucking,
The pipa delivering her endless emotions.
Strings twirl, compress, her fingers flick and smear
First playing "Nishang", and "Liuyao" later.
The thick strings pounding like rapid rain,
The thin strings uttering mellow whispers.
Pounding and whispers, one over another,
Like large, small beads falling on jade plates.
As though warbles came sliding between flowers.
Or quiet springs under ice breaking their way.
Spring water freezes along with the strings
suppressed and frozen, the music halts temporarily,
Perhaps extra sorrow arose from the quietness,
Even more impressive, as she performs silently.
Silver vase abruptly cracks, water bursting out,
And we hear the clashing of armor and weapons.
Music ending, her hand amidst the strings
Like splitting silk, four strings ring in unison.
In the two boats people listened quietly,
Accompanied by the white moon in the river.
She ponders, sliding the pick into the strings,
Straightens her clothes and modifies her demeanor.
“I was a Guh Nyu in the capital,
My home previously under Ha Ma Hill…”
By thirteen she had finished learning Pipa
joined the first orchestra, with her ability.
With every tune she impressed the virtuosos
From peers, her look received every envy.
Gifts to her countless after one tune,
And wealthy teenagers all gathered in line.
Silver combs broken from tapping rhythms,
And her scarlet dress tainted with wine.
Year to year she spent in laughter,
As seasons change, her youth continuously squandered.
Her brother joined the army, auntie deceased,
As time flew by, her appearance withered.
Few appeared at the doorstep, visitors sparse,
Left with no choice, she married a merchant.
He sought for profit, cared not for love,
And last month he left for negotiation.
She remained alone, guarding the empty boat,
Surrounded by the cold river and autumn moon.
Night falls, she suddenly dreamt of her youth,
Awoke in tears, her make-up in ruins.
I already sighed upon hearing the Pipa,
Yet fell into morose hearing her words.
Both lost people, roaming in this world,
How fate has brought us to meet, unreferred!
Since last year, demoted from the capital,
I arrived at Xunyang-city, often fell ill,
The place was remote, without any melodies,
Not even a ring or a trill.
House near the river, low and humid,
Surrounded by sallow reeds and bamboos.
And all the sounds I can hear
Are the cries of monkeys and cuckoos.
These great scenes of rivers and moons,
I viewed with no one as company.
Weren’t there any folk songs and tunes?
There were, but sounded raucous and squawky.
Tonight I heard your Pipa speak,
Enlightening my ears, with tunes from heaven,
Do proceed and play one more,
For you I’ll have “Ode to Pipa” written.
Moved by words, she stood still for long,
Then sat and played, strings tight, pitch elevated.
The music renders poignant yet different,
And we fall into sobs, covering our faces.
To ask who has cried the most-
me, Jiangzhou general, teal shirt soaked with tears.

By Havyn Nguyen, Carmen Ren, and Sifan Wu
